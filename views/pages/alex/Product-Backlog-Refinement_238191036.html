Alex Cook : Product Backlog Refinement

Created by Alex Cook on Mar 03, 2022

An activity that can be used to create alignment on Product Backlog Items or PBIs. This is not a formal Scrum Event because refinement can happen at any time.

We should only refine “just enough”.


Product Backlog Items(PBI) - is a single element of work that exists in the product backlog. PBIs can include user stories, epics, specifications, bugs, or change requirements. In Docent, these are typically Task, Story and Bug tickets in JIRA

Definition of ready - a list of things that can help guide the creation and iteration of PBIs. This should NOT be treated the same as a DoD. They are guidelines and not requirements.

MVP Backlog refinement require only the following:

  • Description
  • Order
  • Estimate
  • Value

Benefits of refinement

  • Increased Transparency
  • Clarification of Value
  • Breaking things down in to consumable pieces
  • Reduction of dependencies
  • Forecasting
  • Incorporation of learning

Building Understanding

Hypothesis Canvas

"We believe we make progress towards the [product goal] when [users] achieve the [outcome] with [feature]."

UX Fishbowl

A UX Fishbowl consists of two groups and two steps, one group being the stakeholders and one being the Scrum Team. The steps are done in a sequence and as many times as needed.

  1. Stakeholders are invited to discuss the questions. "Imagine this feature was already part of the product. How, when, and why would you use it? What steps are involved? What makes it useful to you? What might deter you?" The Scrum Team listens carefully and notes essential insights.
  2. The Scrum Team comes together and formulates follow-up questions based on what they heard. These questions serve as questions for the next round.


Ordering the Backlog

Buy a Feature

  1. Product Backlog items that can be bought are priced and listed.
  2. Money is divided equally among the stakeholders.
  3. Stakeholders buy Product Backlog items that are most important to them. Each item can only be bought once. Stakeholders can combine their money when purchasing.
  4. When all money is spent, the bought items in the list reflect the collectively important items to the stakeholders. 

20/20 Vision 

  1. Each item of the Product Backlog is a separate card.
  2. Cards are shuffled and a face-down pile is formed.
  3. The top card is used as a reference and is placed upside down next to the pile.
  4. After revealing the next card, stakeholders decide whether it is more or less important to them and place it above or below the reference card accordingly.
  5. The process of grouping the remaining cards into the new list is repeated until the last card in the pile. A 20/20 vision of the order of the Product Backlog is created, which shows what is important to stakeholders. 

Estimation

A guess made on the best information available about the size effort and complexity of a PBI

You should assume every estimate might be wrong. If you expect accuracy that can lead to inflated estimates, gaming of numbers, or cutting corners to meet an estimate

Anti-Patterns: Anchoring, Analysis Paralysis…

Techniques

  • Story points
    • 1,2,3,5,8,13…
  • T-Shirting
    • X-Small, Small, Medium, Large, X-Large
  • Animal/Fruits
    • Lime, grapefruit, cantaloupe, watermelon,...
  • Same-size
    • Every ticket is roughly the same size of work
  • Right size
    • Everything is broken down into a ticket that can be delivered within a sprint

Breaking Down PBIs

When breaking down PBIs we must not only focus on getting the ticket to be small enough for down but also that the increment will be valuable

The full team must have a shared understanding of the desired outcomes in able to build the right thing

  • Gives more flexibility to the product owner
  • Stakeholders gain more transparency


Happy vs Unhappy paths

The happy path describes how the functionality will behave if everything goes as desired. If there are deviations, exceptions, or other problems, unhappy paths are invoked.
